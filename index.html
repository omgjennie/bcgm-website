Manifesto

Where it started

Growing up in China, nobody in our house called it TCM. It was just what you did when something felt wrong.

Mom had a routine for everything. When a cold was coming on, it was ginger tea boiled until it burned going down, or banlangen, the bitter root powder every Chinese kid knows the moment a sore throat starts. When something was slightly wrong, skin, digestion, energy, there was always something for it.

None of it felt like medicine at the time. It felt like inherent knowledge, slow, quiet, and trusted because it had been refined over generations.

We grew up in that wisdom without ever needing to name it.

Then we moved to America, and somewhere in the move, we left it behind. Not because we stopped believing in it, but because there was not a version of it that fit into our new lives.

The gap we couldn't unsee

The U.S. wellness market is $480 billion and growing. Ingredients that had lived in Chinese pharmacopoeias for centuries were suddenly showing up at Whole Foods with clean sans-serif branding and clinical copy.

But something important got lost in translation.

They always try to solve each symptom separately: melatonin for sleep, energy products for daytime performance, collagen or whitening supplements for skin, probiotics for digestion, and endless capsules for whatever feels off next.

But these are often surface-level responses to a deeper pattern.

TCM was never built that way. In our thinking, low energy, poor sleep, dull complexion, bloating, sluggish recovery, and lack of radiance are not always separate problems. Those are reflections of a body when its natural rhythm has been disrupted: digestion and metabolism under strain, qi and blood not being sufficiently nourished, and the system losing its ability to restore itself.

Modern life creates these patterns every day: late nights, stress, screens, irregular meals, travel, alcohol, social pressure, overstimulation, and constant productivity.

BCGM is built around the truth most modern wellness ignores:

When the body is not nourished from within, no surface fix can create lasting vitality.

The breakthrough

Lemme built a gummy brand on celebrity and turned a commodity format into a $200 million business. Grüns built a greens gummy brand in 2023 and sold to Unilever for $1.2 billion.

Neither company won on ingredients alone. They won because they understood that supplements only work if people actually take them, and people only consistently take things they genuinely want to.

That is what TCM has never had in the modern consumer market, a format people desire.

The product

BCGM turns real TCM formulas into enjoyable daily products, beginning with sugar-free, genuinely delicious herbal jellies that don't ask people to compromise on taste or convenience.

Why we're doing this

Here's what we keep coming back to: TCM has one of the longest records of human use in wellness history. Thousands of years, the largest population on earth, generation after generation passing down what actually works.

And yet almost none of that trust ever made it onto a shelf modern consumers actually want to shop from.

BCGM is set to change that.

We are here to make 3,000 years of Eastern wellness wisdom globally accessible through products people actually love consuming, and make ancient medicine feel relevant, desirable, and accessible to the modern world.

This is the time.
